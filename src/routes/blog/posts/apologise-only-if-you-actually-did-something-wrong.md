I started thinking about this because people who report to me kept saying:

> Sorry, can I take leave for next Friday?

They were not apologising because they had given no notice, left important work hanging, or caused some operational disaster. They were apologising for wanting to use their leave.

Then I realised that I did it too.

I am not someone who apologises a lot, but even I sometimes say “sorry” when what I mean is “excuse me”. I say it before asking someone to do something that is already part of their job.

Why?

Leave is part of our employment entitlement. Reviewing a legitimate claim is my responsibility. Asking a question is not an offence. Neither is disagreeing with me.

Why do we feel the need to apologise before doing any of these things? And what does that habit do to us?

## Sorry seems to be the easiest word

Elton John sang that _Sorry Seems to Be the Hardest Word_.

At work, it sometimes seems to be the easiest.

> Sorry, can I ask a question?

> Sorry to trouble you, can you approve my claim?

> Sorry, but I disagree.

> Sorry to chase you again.

Nobody consciously means, “I know your time matters more than mine, so please forgive me for requiring your attention.”

Still, that is sometimes the posture we adopt.

One unnecessary apology is not going to make someone weak or passive. But when every ordinary request begins with “sorry”, normal work starts to look like a personal favour.

## Leave is an entitlement, not a confession

Taking leave does not mean abandoning your responsibilities.

Give reasonable notice. Arrange the necessary coverage. Complete or hand over important work. Flag anything that may go wrong while you are away.

If the world will collapse in on itself during your absence, then yes, perhaps we should have a conversation.

I am jesting... mostly.

Entitlement also does not mean that every possible date must work regardless of operational needs. Sometimes there is a genuine conflict that needs to be discussed.

But the normal conversation should sound more like this:

> I plan to take leave next Friday. I have arranged coverage for X and will complete Y before I go. Please flag any operational issue I have missed.

That gives me the information I need without presenting the leave as an offence.

The same applies to claims:

> My claim is ready for review. The receipt and justification are attached.

If reviewing it is part of my role, you are not troubling me. I should review it, approve it if it complies, or explain what is missing.

You do not need to apologise before asking me to do my job.

## Saying sorry changes nothing

There is another side to this. If someone says:

> Sorry, can I take leave next Friday?

the apology does not make their plans more negotiable than they already were. I am not going to hear “sorry” and assume they are inviting me to pressure them into changing their leave.

At the same time, I am not stupid enough to think that saying sorry cushions the blow if they have created a real operational problem.

If they gave no notice, failed to arrange a handover, or left important work hanging, the problem is still there. The apology does not make the situation easier or convince me that it was handled responsibly.

The useful conversation is about what actually matters: whether there was enough notice, whether the work is covered, and whether there is a genuine reason the dates cannot work.

If those things are in order, take the leave. If they are not, let us discuss the actual problem.

Saying sorry should neither weaken your entitlement nor disguise poor planning.

## Is it cultural?

Maybe, at least partly.

My own experience is in the public sector, where hierarchy is visible and politeness matters. But I cannot tell how much of this comes from national culture, public-sector culture, particular workplaces, previous bosses, or ordinary human discomfort with imposing on someone else.

I am not going to turn one workplace habit into a grand theory of Singaporean personality.

The more useful question is what happens when someone speaks directly. If managers treat questions as challenges, disagreement as disrespect, or independent judgement as insubordination, people learn to wrap everything in “sorry”.

## Permission culture creates followers

Managers often say they want people to take ownership.

Then they teach people to seek permission for everything.

Employees learn to ask whether they can send an email, contact someone, raise an issue, make a routine change, or take the next step. Eventually, they stop exercising judgement because waiting is safer.

L. David Marquet writes about replacing permission-seeking with statements of intent.

Compare:

> Can I reject the vendor’s proposal?

with:

> I intend to reject the proposal because it does not meet requirements A and B. I will respond tomorrow unless you see a risk I have missed.

The second person has assessed the issue, explained the reasoning, and given the manager a chance to intervene. Some matters genuinely require approval, and intent is not a licence to ignore controls or act beyond your authority.

How can I expect you to make decisions or try things if I keep teaching you to depend on me?

If a decision genuinely belongs to you, make it. I do not need you to perform ceremonial humility before I accept the responsibility that comes with my role.

## Polite organisations can still be passive

A passive organisation is not necessarily full of passive people. It may be full of capable people who have learnt that acting is dangerous and waiting is safe.

They push uncertainty upwards, managers become bottlenecks, and senior leaders complain that nobody takes ownership.

Language becomes part of the ritual. Every proposal is softened, every concern becomes tentative, and every disagreement begins with “sorry”.

The organisation remains extremely polite while becoming increasingly unable to disagree honestly.

## Sorry as a warning shot

I cannot pretend that all my own apologies come from excessive deference.

Sometimes I know exactly what I am doing. I use “sorry” when I am being paggro. The email is already obviously angry, and I write:

> Sorry, but...

Then I unleash hell. Of course I know the apology is fake. I totally mean everything that comes after it!

It is true... you have definitely received one of my emails like that. The message has been edited just enough to remain professionally survivable, and “sorry” has been placed near the front as though it somehow makes the rest less hostile.

Nobody is fooled.

It is not an apology. It is a warning shot dressed as politeness.

Sometimes I use it because blunt anger feels too naked. “Sorry” gives me a thin layer of cover before I say exactly what I think, even though I have no real intention of softening the message.

That is fake politeness, and I am guilty of it.

I apologise to no one!

## Save apologies for when they have work to do

I am not against apologies.

If you caused harm, broke a commitment, or created avoidable work for someone else, apologise properly. Say what happened, acknowledge the consequence, and explain what you are doing to put it right.

> I made a mistake. I’m sorry. Here is what happened, what I have corrected, and how I will prevent it from happening again.

Keep that apology.

That one has work to do.

Apologies can also communicate genuine empathy. Saying “I’m sorry you are going through this” does not mean you caused the problem. Research has found that expressing regret about an unfortunate situation outside your control can demonstrate concern and increase trust.

The problem is not the word itself.

The problem is using it automatically whenever you need attention, exercise an entitlement, ask a question, express disagreement, or require another person to fulfil a responsibility.

Research on frequent apologising suggests a trade-off. Frequent apologisers may be perceived as warmer and more concerned about relationships. They may also be perceived as less assertive or self-directed, particularly when their apologies appear automatic or insincere.

That does not prove that saying sorry makes you weak.

It does show that apologies carry social meaning, even when we use them without thinking.

Perhaps sorry really should be the hardest word. Not because we should be too proud to say it, but because a genuine apology requires us to admit that we actually did something wrong.

## Respect without submission

None of this means you should become an arsehole. Say please and thank you. Say excuse me when you interrupt someone. Plan your leave properly, and do not use “entitlement” as an excuse to dump work on everyone else.

Do not, however, confuse respect with submission.

Take your leave responsibly. Submit your claim (on time pls). Ask your question. State your disagreement. Tell me what you intend to do.

You do your job. Ask me to do mine.

Neither of us needs to apologise for that.

## What the literature says

The research does not support the simplistic claim that apologising is inherently weak.

Apologies can repair relationships, communicate empathy, and increase trust. In four studies, Alison Wood Brooks, Hengchen Dai, and Maurice Schweitzer found that apologies for unfortunate circumstances outside the speaker’s control could signal empathic concern and increase trust.

The effects become more complicated when apologising is a repeated personal pattern. Karina Schumann and her colleagues found that a person described as a frequent apologiser was judged as more communal, meaning warmer and more relationship-oriented, but also less agentic. In a second study involving romantic relationships, the lower-agency judgement appeared when the frequent apologies were perceived as low quality. This suggests a trade-off rather than a universal rule.

Workplace linguists Janet Holmes and Maria Stubbe describe politeness and power as things people actively perform through everyday workplace conversation. Politeness helps people balance completing work with maintaining collegial relationships. It is not inherently submissive, but it can communicate social distance, authority, and expectations about who may ask whom to do what.

The organisational argument comes from connecting this work with research on employee voice and silence. Elizabeth Morrison’s review shows that leadership, perceived risk, and organisational context influence whether people raise useful information or remain silent. James Detert and Amy Edmondson’s work on implicit voice theories explains how employees develop taken-for-granted beliefs about when speaking is dangerous or inappropriate.

David Marquet’s intent-based leadership is a practical leadership model rather than academic evidence about apologies. It is useful here because it provides an alternative to permission-seeking: people communicate a considered intention to act, while leaders retain the ability to intervene when necessary.

The link to groupthink remains an inference. James Esser’s review found that the groupthink literature had continuing value, but that its empirical base was relatively small and many theoretical ideas remained untested. There is no demonstrated causal chain from saying “sorry” to organisational passivity or groupthink.

My argument is narrower: routine apologies can be both a symptom and a small reinforcing practice within a deferential culture. They repeatedly frame normal questions, entitlements, decisions, and requests as impositions requiring forgiveness or permission.
