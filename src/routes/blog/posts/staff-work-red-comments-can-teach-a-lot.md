The [first](/blog/staff-work-make-it-make-sense-before-it-goes-up) and [second](/blog/staff-work-small-stuff-is-still-part-of-staff-work) cover doing the work and preparing the package. # Red comments can teach a lot, if the officer looks

A document covered in comments, tracked changes and strikethroughs can tell an officer a lot about what good looks like. They can see what you removed, which point you moved forward, where you narrowed a claim and how you made the recommendation clearer.

Some officers study the edits and apply the lessons to their next piece of work. Others click "accept all" and move on (at times in the past that's what I did). The next submission then arrives with the exact same problems.

So yes, red comments can teach a lot. They just cannot teach someone who doesn't care enough to look at them.

This post covers both sides of that exchange: how officers should interpret comments, and how managers can help the lesson transfer to the next piece of work.

## A. When someone comments on your work

### Look at the context, not just the words

When someone asks a question or comments on a submission, consider:

- What is the context of the question?
- Does the person have some larger context I am not aware of?
- Is there some aspect of the work that could be improved beyond the explicit call-out?
- Is the issue with the phrasing, nuancing, examples, clarity, evidence or how much detail we gave?
- Is the person genuinely unsure, or trying to spur us to think in a certain direction?
- Am I about to become very extra and infer an entire hidden agenda?

Sometimes the words contain the whole question. Sometimes they are pointing towards a concern that the person has not fully spelt out.

### "Why did we not grant this?"

Suppose your boss asks:

> Why did we not grant this?

The immediate answer may be:

> The request didn't meet criterion X.

Ya it's technically correct. But also begs many more questions. Are we applying the criterion too tightly? Have we granted similar cases before? Is the refusal defensible? Does the person suffer a significant disadvantage? Did anybody screw up earlier in the process?

I would probably check the precedent and the implications, then reply along these lines:

> We didn't grant it because the request fell outside criterion X. I checked the comparable cases. We granted one earlier exception because Y applied, which is different from this case. The refusal is consistent with our usual position, although it creates disadvantage Z. I didn't find a processing error. If the concern is that criterion X has become too tight, we should review that separately.

Sometimes the boss genuinely wanted only the surface reason. That's alright. You should still consider the surrounding issues before deciding how much to say.

### A small comment can expose a bigger problem

A question about one statistic may mean the wider claim has no real evidence. "Can we say this?" may mean the sentence is too absolute, or the reader knows of an exception that you missed.

The improvement could be somewhere other than the exact sentence that attracted the comment. Maybe the example is poor. Maybe the paper has become too convoluted. Maybe the recommendation needs more nuance or the evidence belongs in the main text.

Don't treat every comment like a ticket where your job is to fill in one field and send it back.

### Reply with the answer and the edit

Where useful, answer the question and update the work at the same time.

For example:

> The phrasing was because X. I agree with your comment that Y, and have incorporated the relevant cases from the last decade (highlighted in yellow). The overall recommendation remains.

### The suggested words are not always the fix

Bosses sometimes type a proposed sentence because they are thinking aloud. Their wording may solve the concern, or it may create a new problem elsewhere. You usually know the detailed context better because you spent days on the work and they read it between meetings.

Understand what they care about, then make the best amendment. A clear instruction remains a clear instruction, but every comment is not necessarily dictation.

Clarify when your interpretation would change the recommendation, create a lot of additional work or send the paper in a completely different direction. For smaller choices, use your judgement and move on.

### Don't go completely overboard

The think-ahead habit has a downside. Sometimes the boss wants a few more stats or is jumpy, but otherwise there's nothing really wrong. So no need to over compensate. Tweak where you need, stay calm, clarify when in doubt (before you run off doing tons of work), and manage expectations if you must.

You will occasionally answer too narrowly, and on another day become very kiasu and do too much. Experience is partly remembering which way you overdid it last time.

## B. For managers

### See whether anything transfers

The next piece of work tells you whether the lesson landed. Did the officer put the point first this time? Did they check the obvious precedent? Are they still compiling everybody's input into one long paper?

A corrected document may look excellent because the manager effectively rewrote it. That doesn't mean the officer can now produce the same quality independently.

If the same issue appears for the fourth time, leaving the same comment for the fifth time is probably not the answer.

### Sometimes edit it together

Depending on the officer, sit down and edit the work together in real time.

This lets the officer see the choices as you make them. Why did you move the conclusion forward? Why was the technically correct answer still inadequate? Why did you remove half the background? What did you think the boss was actually asking?

It also lets you see how the officer was thinking. Maybe they misunderstood the assignment. Maybe they could not tell which facts mattered. They may have rushed, or thought that compiling all the input was what you wanted. Sometimes they simply didn't care enough, which requires a different conversation from another lesson on staff work.

There is no need to explain every comma. Spend the time on the judgement that can be reused later.

### Match the guidance to the person

Some officers learn well from written comments. Another may need to talk through the issue before drafting. Someone else understands the principle but struggles when the case is politically sensitive.

Newer officers may need examples and closer direction. Others can work from a broad steer. Giving everybody the same amount and type of supervision is easier for the manager, but it is not always useful.

### Give more room as judgement improves

Officers need opportunities to make decisions and occasionally get them wrong. Give them enough context, explain the important boundaries, then let them own the work.

Autonomy doesn't mean sending somebody away with a vague instruction and appearing again at the deadline to criticise the result. It also doesn't mean taking the work back the moment the officer approaches it differently from you.

### Admit when your comments/guidance was wrong

Managers sometimes infer what a senior person wants and guide the team accordingly. Sometimes that inference is completely off.

I've done this. Tell the team, own the mistake and correct the work. Don't quietly rewrite the history so that the officer appears to have misunderstood an instruction that came from you.

### Add value instead of becoming another clearance layer

A manager should help the officer think. Share context the officer could not reasonably know, explain what the senior decision-maker is sensitive to, and help them work out whether an issue needs more analysis or simply a decision.

Share useful lessons across the team too. Everybody should not have to discover the same thing separately.

Eventually, the officer should be able to produce the work without you rewriting everything. Otherwise, you are doing two jobs and developing nobody.
