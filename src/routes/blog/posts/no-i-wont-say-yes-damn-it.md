Our recent Organisational Climate Survey surfaced concerns about yes-man culture in our organisation. We work within that culture, so none of us are immune to it.

Telling people to "speak up" is too simplistic. Hierarchy and office politics are real. What helps is knowing how to read the room, introduce disagreement, find support, and decide when to push.

## How to spot it

I am still amazed by how often this happens:

- In meetings chaired by very senior leaders, people who know little about the subject agree wholeheartedly, then change their tune depending on where the conversation lands.
- People in high-level forums swallow their disagreements and say yes whenever they are asked whether something can or should be done.
- Someone says, "<senior person> wants it, so don’t ask so much. Just do it."
- Leaders say they are uncomfortable proposing something that goes against what a bigger boss said previously.
- A throwaway comment made by <senior person> years ago gets repeated as if it were policy or sacrosanct truth.

The common thread is that authority replaces judgement. People stop asking whether the direction still makes sense and start working out how to comply.

## How I became the devil’s advocate

I have become the devil’s advocate dialled way up.

This happened mostly by accident. When I was junior, I was very accommodating whenever senior people gave instructions or "guidance".

A few years in, during skip-level meetings, I started raising things that other people wanted to say but did not dare to, or viewpoints that seemed obvious to me but somehow had not been said. I have also always been a bit contrarian in life, so saying "just to play devil’s advocate" gave me an easy way into the discussion.

It softened the impact because I framed the disagreement as helping them red-team the idea.

After a while, it became a habit. I would try to say something different from everyone else in the room, and people started appreciating that I covered blind spots they had missed.

Over time, it became my brand. If I spoke, it was usually to contradict something or offer another way of looking at it.

I say shit that does not stick all the time too. Sometimes everyone is just puzzled because my thinking went so far off the edge it fell off a frigging cliff.

I think that is part and parcel of being contrarian and occasionally having valuable input. Sometimes it sounds stupid, and sometimes it actually is stupid. That is okay.

## Give yourself a way into the conversation

When I was more junior, phrases like these helped:

- "Just to play devil’s advocate..."
- "Could we explore...?"
- "Someone might question..."
- "How might we reply if someone challenged us on this?"
- "Is there any evidence that could possibly change our minds?"

The phrasing is deliberately soft. It gets the issue into the room without forcing everyone to immediately choose a side.

Nowadays I am older and too tired or lazy to hide it, so I usually just say that I disagree, explain why, and launch into the logic, evidence, experience, industry practice, or technical basis behind my view.

I can do that because I have spent years building trust and some image of competence. Directness lands differently when people know that you have done the work and are trying to improve the decision.

Junior officers may need more scaffolding. Use the softer entry, then make the substance clear.

## Use evidence, experience, and principles

Being contrarian without doing the work becomes tiring very quickly.

If you challenge something, understand the issue well enough to explain the assumption, the evidence, and what could happen if the assumption is wrong.

My time in SNDGO helped a lot. We saw many agencies, recurring problems, and different attempts to solve the same things. I can now say that I have seen a particular approach fail elsewhere, or that another agency faced the same constraint and dealt with it differently.

That gives the argument more credibility than a personal opinion.

This advantage will expire. Knowledge spreads and contexts change, so examples become less useful with time. You need to keep learning or your experience eventually becomes another old story people repeat without checking whether it still applies.

I also fall back on a few basic principles:

- Public money should solve a real problem.
- Technical decisions should involve people who understand the subject.
- The person accepting a risk should understand and own it.
- Decisions that are difficult to reverse need stronger evidence.
- Senior intent matters more than preserving an old instruction forever.

Principles help when the evidence remains incomplete. They also make the disagreement less personal. The question becomes whether the proposal meets the standard, rather than whose rank carries more weight.

## Find someone who can give you air cover

Some voices carry more weight than others. That is just how organisations work.

A few former bosses used small pre-meeting briefs to align everyone. We would address concerns candidly, settle the major issues, then enter the larger forum broadly aligned.

This creates additional work because someone has to arrange the meeting, prepare the material, and get people to attend. The larger meeting usually becomes much more productive because the difficult thinking has already happened.

It also gives senior sponsors a chance to understand your position before they are asked to respond publicly. Sometimes you need them to back you openly. Other times you only need them to ask one question that gives everyone else permission to speak.

I used this route for the static-site issue I wrote about previously. The website owner strongly resisted moving away from an expensive vendor arrangement. We pushed, escalated privately, and made sure senior people had the full picture. They agreed with the argument, we went all the way, and the owner eventually migrated.

They did not credit our resistance as the reason, and that was fine. The better outcome mattered more.

## Work out why people are resisting

Disagreements usually involve more than the facts in front of us.

People may be invested in the existing direction. They may want to avoid additional work or lack the knowledge to evaluate the issue. Others stick to custom because it matches their worldview or principles, while some simply have little appetite for change.

That changes how you should respond.

A knowledge gap may need examples and evidence. Avoidance of work needs a clear account of what inaction will cost. Tradition requires a discussion about whether the original reason still applies. Low appetite for change may require someone senior to create permission.

Sometimes none of this works. The onus then falls on the people who care about the change to keep leading it, find allies, or accept that the organisation is choosing the existing path.

## Know when to stop

I will usually try a few times. If the issue matters, I do escalate it privately and make sure senior folks understand the concern.

If the person in charge and all the relevant senior people still do not want to move, I cannot force the issue. So be it.

I also do not usually go out of my way to create a written record of every disagreement. It feels a bit chicken to send something mainly to protect yourself later.

That said, I have sometimes been thankful that an email trail existed when a disagreement became ugly. A record can preserve what everyone understood at the time, even when that was never its intended purpose.

There is a point where the decision belongs to the person with authority. You have raised the issue, explained the consequence, and given them the chance to decide with the full picture.

You still control where you choose to be. You can leave.

## The cost of being this person

People have called me abrasive and blunt. Some hate working with me, and many probably find me exhausting. I am sure the people managing me sometimes find me a pain.

My teammates may also feel frustrated that their boss does not always have the best relationships with people who can open doors. Sometimes it probably looks like I am shutting those doors instead.

That's a real cost.

I see it as part and parcel of staying true to the interests that matter, which are the citizen, public money, and our country. It is also being true to myself and saying what I genuinely think.

It just so happens that this approach has generally brought me recognition in the positive sense. That outcome was never guaranteed, and I would still have to decide what I was willing to live with if it had gone the other way.

I can try a few times, build the argument, and make sure the people with authority know what I think. After that, they own the decision.

I still own where I choose to work.

## What the literature calls it

The research on **employee voice** and **organisational silence** explains one important part of yes-man culture: why people withhold concerns or avoid challenging authority.

**Employee silence** is the withholding of work-related ideas, information, concerns, or opinions. **Organisational silence** is the broader pattern that develops when this withholding becomes widespread and people share the belief that speaking up is dangerous or pointless.

Researchers distinguish several reasons for silence. People may remain quiet because they have resigned themselves to the situation, because they want to protect themselves, or because they believe withholding information will protect other people or the organisation.

There is also research on **implicit voice theories**: the unwritten assumptions people carry about when speaking up is dangerous or inappropriate. Someone may believe they should not challenge a boss without complete evidence, bypass the chain of command, embarrass a manager publicly, or question something associated with a senior leader.

**Team psychological safety** describes whether team members share the belief that they can speak up with ideas, questions, concerns, or mistakes without expecting humiliation or punishment. It does not mean that everyone agrees or that every suggestion is accepted. It creates the conditions for candour, learning, and constructive disagreement.

That broadly matches what I have seen. People stay quiet because they expect a cost, believe speaking will achieve nothing, or assume that challenging the established position will be treated as criticism of the person who owns it.

The research also supports parts of the experience I described above. Managers may react less favourably to voice that challenges the existing direction than to suggestions that support it, partly because challenging voice can be perceived as less loyal or more threatening. That helps explain why questioning the status quo can carry interpersonal costs.

In my experience, preparation, framing, and credibility can reduce those costs. That is my interpretation rather than a direct finding from the research.

There is also evidence for deliberately introducing disagreement into decision-making. A 1990 meta-analysis found that devil’s advocacy generally produced better decisions than a no-conflict expert approach, although the evidence for other structured-conflict methods was less consistent. This concerns a formal method of testing an argument, not the idea that every contrarian opinion is automatically useful.

A related concept is the **Abilene paradox**, where people collectively support a course of action that many of them privately oppose because each person incorrectly assumes that the others want it. It is a useful description of how groups can produce apparent agreement without genuine individual support, although it comes from a conceptual management article rather than the stronger empirical literature on voice and silence.
