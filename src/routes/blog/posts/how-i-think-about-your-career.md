In my last essay I tried to be transparent about how I assess people. This one is the natural follow-up, how I think about your career specifically, what development actually looks like in a place like ours, and some things I wish someone had told me earlier.

I've told various people parts of this in different conversations over the years, so here's the unabridged version. As before, I'm still figuring a lot of this out myself, so take what's useful and leave the rest.

## The scholar myth, and why I've moved past it

I'm a scholar. A mid-term local scholar, which if you know how the hierarchy works, puts me fairly close to the bottom of that ladder. I mention this because I think it matters for what comes next and because I'd rather be upfront about it.

The system gives scholars opportunities for three reasons: (a) it's ROI on public money, (b) it's a reasonable prior that they're smart, and (c) it's SOP. That's the honest version. However, it's also a blunt heuristic, and heuristics are wrong often enough that I've stopped trusting this one.

I came in with a structural advantage that most people around me (including people on this team) didn't have, and I'd rather say that plainly than pretend otherwise. But I also want to be honest about what that advantage actually bought me: a foot in the door, an assumption of competence, early investment, and that was about it.

When I arrived at a previous job, my credentials turned out to matter less than I'd expected. I could do the work itself, but I lacked the domain knowledge that would have made me relevant to the people I was working with. The label couldn't fill that gap, and because people assume the scholar has it together, the struggle stayed invisible.

I've seen the system get it wrong in both directions enough times that I genuinely don't use it as a signal when I assess people. What actually predicts whether someone is valuable is closer to what I wrote about in the last essay, which were impact, drive, expertise, heart. What you do once you're inside tells me everything I need to know.

## What "career development" means in a place like ours

In most organisations, career development is shorthand for moving up. In a large bureaucratic org with scheme constraints, scheme ceilings, and limited positions, that framing sets people up for disappointment, so I'd rather be honest about that than sell a story that doesn't match reality.

Development here more often looks like getting broader, getting deeper, or getting more relevant to people who matter. Vertical movement is one of several possible outcomes.

The DXO scheme has real structural constraints I can't paper over. There are fewer positions than there are capable people, and some ceilings are beyond what I can raise. I'll always tell you which battles I can fight and which ones are out of my hands, because false reassurance helps nobody.

What I think is genuinely underrated is growing inside your current role. Most people hit a level of comfort and plateau there, and the plateau is usually more specific than laziness, because most people work hard. It's the moment when being comfortable becomes the goal in itself. The tell, which I've seen inside and outside the department, is usually resistance to changing something you've always done a certain way, even when the environment has clearly moved on. That's inertia dressed up as experience, and it looks quite different from principled pushback.

There's usually a harder, more valuable version of what you're already doing. Finding that version is often where real development happens, and most people move on before they've fully extracted what their current role had to teach them.

The newly reorganised structure we're in also means some things are still settling. That's unsettling, but it's an opportunity too. New departments need people to define what good looks like, and that window doesn't stay open forever.

## The three tracks are equal, but you have to believe that too

Planning, development, maintenance and support. I see these as equal. However, that only matters if the culture around you signals the same thing, and I know the org naturally glamourises new things. A shiny new project catches eyes faster than something running cleanly in the background, and I'm part of that instinct too.

So let me be specific about what good actually looks like in each track, because vague reassurance doesn't help anyone understand what they're being assessed on.

**Planning and strategy** is about connecting dots across the org early enough to matter, translating ambiguity from above into something the ground can actually act on, and knowing when to push back on a direction and having the credibility to do it. The failure mode is producing beautiful documents that sit on a shelf.

**Development** is about pushing new things through a system that wasn't built to move fast, because that's the actual job, and the project itself is just the vehicle. It means managing vendors and stakeholders without losing the plot or getting captured by either, and knowing when to kill something, because that matters just as much as knowing how to launch it. The failure mode is shipping for the sake of shipping, or getting so attached to a project you stop being objective about it.

**Maintenance and support** is where system knowledge lives and compounds over time. Knowing why a system was built a certain way, what was tried before, where the bodies are buried... that expertise is hard to replace and often the most valuable thing in a room during a crisis. Being the person who catches something before it becomes a crisis is invisible until the day it saves everyone. The failure mode is following SOP without understanding why the SOP exists (more on why that matters in a moment).

What I'd push back on is using your track as an explanation for limited growth, because the ceiling in any track is higher than most people test. If you want to move tracks, that's a legitimate conversation to have with me, but be honest with yourself about whether curiosity is pulling you forward or discomfort is pushing you away from where you are.

## Job rotation: what it can and what it can't do for you

Rotation broadens you and breadth matters. I've said that in my assessment framework and I mean it.

That said, I've also watched people treat rotation as a career reset button. Jumping tracks without genuine curiosity about the new domain usually just means you bring your old habits somewhere new. The accumulated context, relationships, and credibility you've built in your current role took time, and leaving it behind has a cost that people underestimate.

We're also a newly formed department still finding our shape, so some instability is normal and expected. Rotating out of anxiety about that instability is probably the wrong reason to move.

The honest question to ask yourself is "what am I specifically trying to learn, and is this the best way to learn it." If you can answer the first part clearly, the second part becomes a real conversation we can have. If you can't, it's worth sitting with that a bit longer.

I'm not going to push rotation as a panacea. The org offers it, and it can be valuable, but the initiative and the clarity of purpose have to come from you. Come talk to me if you're thinking about it, because I'd rather have that conversation early than after you've already decided.

## Tech is a different kind of job, and yes-man culture is especially dangerous here

In most jobs, following SOP faithfully is neutral at worst. In tech, it can be actively dangerous. The threat landscape, the tooling, the best practices shift faster than any SOP can track, and we exist in this organisation because we know things others don't, can see problems they can't, and are willing to say uncomfortable things. The moment we become order-takers who defer everything upward or rubber-stamp vendor decisions without scrutiny, we've lost our reason for existing.

If you're current, you're useful, and if you've fallen behind, that shows faster in tech than anywhere else. Staying current requires deliberate effort because it doesn't happen by just doing your job. That's a hard thing to say and I mean it.

But knowledge is only part of it. You also need the experience to know when something is off, when a new thing is really just an old problem in new clothes, when to push and when to hold. That judgment only comes from time and from caring enough to pay attention, which is why I ask what people have been learning lately, whether they tinker outside office hours, how they keep up. It tells me something real.

Let me give a concrete example. Someone I know recently encountered a proposal to use AI to scan code for vulnerabilities, and their instinct was to reject it. The reasoning, when you dug into it, was about remediation: if the scan finds bugs, we have to fix them, and that creates work and accountability.

But that logic only holds if you believe that skipping the scan makes the bugs disappear. Bad actors find vulnerabilities on their own schedule. The instinct to avoid the scan was really an instinct to avoid accountability, dressed up as caution, and in security that kind of thinking can cost you everything.

I understand where the instinct comes from, because surfacing problems that become your problems to fix is uncomfortable. But that's exactly the job. If you're only comfortable raising good news, you are genuinely the wrong person for a technical role. I say that with care and I mean it completely.

Yes-man culture in a normal job means missed opportunities. In tech it means unpatched vulnerabilities, bad vendor decisions that went unquestioned, and systems that fail in ways everyone could have predicted.

## The risk-aversion trap

Risk-aversion is rational in many parts of life, and this org selects for it. Stable people, steady performers, long tenures. I understand why the culture is what it is.

I'll be honest, I've caught myself wanting credit for things I hadn't fully delivered yet. It's a very human instinct. But recognition rarely comes before the risk, and waiting for it usually means waiting forever.

The plateau pattern I mentioned earlier is often risk-aversion in disguise. Most people work hard, so the issue is more specific than laziness. It's the discomfort of doing something before you're ready, of being visibly uncertain, of possibly failing in front of people whose opinion matters to you.

There's a difference between reckless risk-taking and calibrated discomfort, and I'm asking for the latter. Staying entirely within your comfort zone has a cost that compounds quietly and shows up later when you wonder why growth stopped.

The org will push you to deliver, but it won't push you out of your comfort zone. That initiative has to come from you.

And if you try something and it doesn't work, I'd rather know about it. Failure that you're honest about is something I can work with, but failure that gets quietly shelved is how small problems become large ones.

## Things I'd tell myself and anyone who wants to listen

Know your stuff, because it's the only thing that makes you genuinely useful to the people around you. Credentials, tenure, title, none of it substitutes for relevance.

Dig deeper into what you're already doing before you look for something new. Most roles have more to teach than people give them credit for, and the ones who extract that value are the ones who grow fastest.

Say the uncomfortable thing early. It gets harder the longer you wait, and the cost of waiting is usually higher than the cost of saying it.

Hold the system's assessment of you loosely, because labels are blunt instruments. You know more about what you're capable of than any scheme or hierarchy does, and that cuts both ways.

Growth is uncomfortable by definition. If everything feels manageable, you're probably coasting.

And if you're struggling, say so, to me or to someone. The people who look like they have it together mostly just got better at hiding it. I spent a long time in that category, and it helped me less than I thought it would.

I run a half-yearly poll, a venting machine form for ad hoc feedback, and I speak to everyone individually at mid-year and during performance appraisal. I genuinely read everything that comes through them. However, I'm also aware that forms and scheduled conversations catch the things people are comfortable enough to write down or say in a structured setting. The smaller, earlier signal, the thing that's been quietly bothering someone for three weeks, often doesn't make it that far.

That's the gap I'm still figuring out how to close. 🪴
